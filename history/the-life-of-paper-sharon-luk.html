The Life of Paper offers a wholly original and inspiring analysis of how letter correspondence, a central experience of incarceration, has facilitated a form of communal life for groups targeted for racialized confinement across the development of the U.S. West. Providing an ambitiously comparative and connected history of the detention of Southern Chinese migrants (1880s–1920s), the internment of Japanese Americans (1930s–1940s), and the mass incarceration of African Americans (1960s–present), Luk explores imprisonment as a way of life. She examines the social lives constructed through these letters and correspondences among the incarcerated that reinvent people’s means of living. From bodily integrity to subjectivity to collective and spiritual being, letter writing provides a glimpse into how communities under constraint remake themselves.
